#LegalDocs — Phase 4: RAG Risk Analyser (Core Phase)

Tech stack: Node.js / TypeScript, OpenAI, PostgreSQL + pgvector (Phase 0/1), LangChain.js, LangGraph.js — advanced usage (conditional edges, Send, checkpointers, time travel), @langchain/langgraph-checkpoint-postgres Depends on: Phase 1 (legal_reference_chunks — the knowledge base this phase retrieves from), Phase 2 (contract_type), Phase 3 (the clauses table — this phase's unit of work) Feeds into: Phase 4.5 (the judgment_lookup stub built here becomes the Legal Research Agent's home); Phase 5 (the Report Generator reads risk_findings); Phase 6.5 (this entire graph gets wrapped as a subgraph under a Supervisor)

#1. Phase Overview

#Objective

For every clause Phase 3 extracted, produce a risk finding: a risk level (LOW/MEDIUM/HIGH), a numeric score, a plain-language explanation grounded in your Phase 1 legal knowledge base, a concrete fix suggestion, and references to the specific statute/judgment excerpts that justify the assessment. All of this must happen in parallel across every clause of a contract, not one at a time, and every run must be checkpointed — persisted step by step — so a compliance reviewer can later inspect exactly how a finding was reached, and correct it without re-running the entire analysis from scratch.

This is the phase where the project stops being "a few LLM calls chained together" and becomes a real orchestrated system: a graph with conditional branching, dynamic parallel fan-out over a runtime-determined list of clauses, and durable, inspectable state.

#Why this phase is important

This is your product's core value proposition — everything before it (parsing, classification, clause extraction) exists to feed this phase, and everything after it (the research agent, the report, the multi-agent refactor) exists to enrich or present this phase's output. Two things make it disproportionately harder than everything so far, and both get solved here:

  1. Retrieval quality directly determines answer quality. A risk finding is only as trustworthy as the legal text it's grounded in. Phase 2.5's plain similarity search was good enough for quick contract Q&A, but a risk assessment feeding a real compliance decision deserves better: diverse, non-redundant context (MMR), robustness to a vaguely-worded clause not matching the knowledge base's phrasing (Multi-Query), and a final context window that isn't padded with irrelevant boilerplate the model has to wade through (Contextual Compression).
  2. A sequential loop over clauses doesn't scale, and a plain LCEL chain has no way to express "run N independent things, then converge, and remember exactly what happened." This phase is where LangGraph's more advanced primitives — Send()-based dynamic fan-out, conditional edges, and checkpointers — stop being nice-to-know and become the only reasonable way to build the feature at all.

#2. Concept Learning

#2.1 Retrievers, part two: from "similar" to "good"

Phase 2.5 built the simplest possible retriever: embed the query, return the top-k nearest chunks by cosine distance. That's a fine starting point, but it has three real weaknesses once the stakes are "justify a legal risk assessment" rather than "answer a quick question":

Weakness 1 — near-duplicate results. A statute is often paraphrased across multiple chunks (an amendment, a commentary, a related section) — plain similarity search can return five chunks that are all slightly different phrasings of the same point, wasting your top-k budget instead of surfacing genuinely different, complementary information.

Weakness 2 — vocabulary mismatch. A clause might say "the Vendor shall not be held responsible for indirect losses," while the relevant statute uses "consequential damages" and "limitation of liability." A single embedding of the clause's exact wording can under-retrieve the right statute simply because the phrasing doesn't overlap much in vector space, even though the legal concept is identical.

Weakness 3 — noisy context. Even a well-retrieved chunk might be 80% preamble/definitions and 20% the actually relevant sentence. Stuffing the full chunk into the risk-scoring prompt wastes tokens and can dilute the model's attention away from the part that matters.

Each of the three retriever upgrades below solves exactly one of these three weaknesses, and — this is the important design idea — they compose, each one wrapping the previous as a decorator, so by the end you have one retriever object that solves all three at once without any single piece being complicated on its own.

#2.2 MMR (Maximal Marginal Relevance) — solving weakness 1

Plain similarity search asks one question: "which candidates are closest to the query?" MMR asks a second question at the same time: "...and which of those candidates are meaningfully different from each other?"

The algorithm: fetch a larger candidate pool than you actually need (say, fetchK = 20 candidates for a final k = 5), then select iteratively. At each step, pick the candidate that maximizes:

text
MMR_score(doc) = λ × similarity(doc, query) − (1 − λ) × max(similarity(doc, already_selected))

In plain language: "prefer documents that are relevant to the query, but penalize a document for being too similar to something you've already picked." λ (lambda) controls the trade-off — λ = 1 is plain similarity search (no diversity penalty at all), λ = 0 maximizes diversity regardless of relevance. A λ around 0.5–0.7 is a reasonable default: still clearly relevance-driven, but no longer willing to return five near-identical restatements of the same point.

Concrete example: a query about "limitation of liability" might have its top-5 nearest neighbors all be paragraphs from the same Indian Contract Act section, each slightly reworded across different chunking passes. MMR would keep the single best one and use its remaining slots on the genuinely distinct related material (a relevant judgment, a different but related statute section) instead of four more near-copies.

#2.3 Multi-Query Retriever — solving weakness 2

Instead of trusting a single embedding of the clause text to find the right vocabulary match, ask an LLM to generate several different phrasings of the same underlying question, retrieve for each phrasing independently, and take the union of results (deduplicated).

text
Original clause text → LLM generates 3 reformulations:
  1. "limitation of liability for indirect and consequential damages"
  2. "vendor exclusion of responsibility for indirect losses"
  3. "cap on damages recoverable under a service contract"
→ retrieve (base + MMR) for each phrasing independently
→ union + dedupe the results by chunk id

This directly targets weakness 2: even if the clause's exact wording doesn't overlap well with the statute's phrasing, it's very likely that at least one of the 3-4 LLM-generated reformulations will land closer to how the relevant statute or judgment actually describes the concept. The cost is straightforward: N reformulations means N retrieval calls instead of one (though each individual retrieval call itself is cheap — it's an embedding + a vector search, not a full LLM call).

#2.4 Contextual Compression Retriever — solving weakness 3

This wraps a retriever with a compression step: after retrieval returns a batch of candidate documents, before they're handed to the final risk-scoring prompt, run each one through a small extraction step that asks "given this specific query, what part (if any) of this document is actually relevant?" — keeping only that excerpt, or dropping the document entirely if nothing in it is relevant after all (which can happen: a document can rank in the top-k by overall similarity while still not containing anything genuinely on-point for this specific question).

This is a genuinely different operation from summarization — it's extraction, not paraphrase. You want the model finding and returning the actual relevant sentences, not writing a shorter version of the whole document. The value: your final risk-scoring prompt sees dense, on-topic legal text instead of full chunks padded with irrelevant surrounding material, which both reduces token cost and measurably improves how well the model can reason about what it's been given.

#2.5 Composing the retriever stack

Because every retriever in LangChain shares the same interface (invoke(query) → Document[], from Phase 2.5's Concept Learning 2.4), each of these upgrades can be a decorator — a class that holds a reference to an "inner" retriever, calls it, and post-processes the result — without any of them needing to know or care what's inside the one they're wrapping.

text
ContextualCompressionRetriever
   └── wraps → MultiQueryRetriever
                   └── wraps → MmrRetriever
                                   └── wraps → base LegalKnowledgeBaseRetriever
                                                   (plain cosine similarity + doc_group filter,
                                                    same shape as Phase 2.5's ContractRetriever)

Calling .invoke(clauseText) on the outermost object transparently runs the whole stack: generate reformulations → retrieve + MMR-rerank for each → union/dedupe → compress each surviving document down to its relevant excerpt. This is the concrete payoff of building the "basic" retriever by hand in Phase 2.5 rather than reaching for an opaque wrapper immediately — every layer here is legible because you already understand the layer underneath it.

#2.6 Send() — dynamic parallel fan-out over a runtime-determined list

Phase 3's extract_clauses node used a plain Promise.all inside one node to process a known, already-loaded list of windows concurrently — that works because the node itself decided to parallelize its own internal work, and the graph never saw more than one "extraction" step happen.

This phase needs something structurally different: the graph itself needs to dynamically create one execution of a node per clause, where the number of clauses isn't known until the contract's clause list is loaded from the database at runtime. This is exactly what Send() is for.

ts
function dispatchClauseScoring(state: AnalysisState): Send[] {
  return state.clauses.map(
    (clause) => new Send("score_clause", { currentClause: clause })
  );
}

graph.addConditionalEdges("resolve_doc_groups", dispatchClauseScoring);

Instead of returning the name of a single next node (like a normal conditional edge would), dispatchClauseScoring returns an array of Send objects — each one says "run the node called score_clause, but override its input state with this specific clause." LangGraph spawns one parallel execution of score_clause per Send object, each with a different currentClause value layered on top of the shared graph state. This is the direct, graph-native analogue of Phase 3's Promise.all — but expressed as a first-class part of the graph's structure (traceable, checkpointable, and composable with conditional routing per branch) rather than hidden inside one node's implementation.

This exact shape — "map a node over a dynamically-sized list, then converge" — is the map-reduce pattern, and it's the standard way LangGraph handles "I don't know how many parallel branches I need until runtime."

#2.7 Conditional edges and the Command object

You've already used one conditional construct — Phase 2's RunnableBranch — for routing inside an LCEL chain. LangGraph's equivalent, addConditionalEdges, does the same job at the graph level: a routing function inspects the current state and returns the name of whichever node should run next.

ts
function routeByRisk(state: AnalysisState): string {
  return state.currentFinding?.riskLevel === "HIGH" ? "judgment_lookup" : "store_finding";
}

graph.addConditionalEdges("score_clause", routeByRisk);

A Command object is a slightly more powerful alternative a node can return instead of a plain partial-state object: it lets a single node's return value carry both a state update and an explicit instruction for where to go next, in one place, rather than splitting "what changed" (the node's return) and "where to go" (a separate conditional-edge function) across two different pieces of code. This phase uses the separate-function style (addConditionalEdges) for routeByRisk because the routing logic here is simple enough to read clearly as its own function — Command becomes more valuable once a node's own internal logic is what determines routing dynamically and cleanly co-locating both makes the code easier to follow, which you'll see more of once Phase 6.5's Supervisor needs to decide routing based on more complex internal reasoning.

#2.8 The map-reduce join: how parallel branches converge

Once score_clause has run once per clause (some routing on to judgment_lookup first, others going straight to store_finding), you need all of those independent branches to converge back into one place before the graph can produce a final, complete result. In LangGraph, this happens naturally: if multiple nodes each have an edge pointing to the same downstream node, the graph waits until every currently-active branch has completed its current step before running that shared downstream node — it does not run the shared node once per incoming branch. Combined with a concatenating reducer (the same pattern from Phase 3's rawExtractedClauses field), this means the shared aggregate_findings node sees the complete list of findings from every parallel branch, exactly once, having correctly waited for all of them.

text
score_clause (×N, parallel)
   ├── HIGH   → judgment_lookup → store_finding ──┐
   └── other  → store_finding ─────────────────────┼──► aggregate_findings (runs once, after ALL branches arrive)
                                                     │
                                                    END

#2.9 Checkpointers — why a graph run needs to be persisted, not just executed

Everything so far in this project has been "stateless": an HTTP request comes in, a chain or graph runs, a result goes back out, and nothing about the run itself survives past the response. That's fine for a single LLM call, but a full contract risk analysis can involve dozens of parallel branches, take real wall-clock time, and — critically for a legal tool — needs to be auditable after the fact: a compliance reviewer should be able to open a completed analysis days later and see exactly what happened at every step, not just the final answer.

A checkpointer solves this by saving the graph's state to persistent storage after every "superstep" (roughly: after every node or parallel batch of nodes finishes). PostgresSaver (from @langchain/langgraph-checkpoint-postgres) does this directly into your existing Postgres database — no new infrastructure, consistent with your Phase 1 reasoning for choosing pgvector over a separate vector database.

Every checkpointed run is identified by a thread_id — a string you choose (here, a UUID per analysis run) that groups all of a single run's checkpoints together, the same way a contract_id groups a contract's clauses. You pass it via the config object on every call:

ts
const config = { configurable: { thread_id: analysisRunId } };
await compiledGraph.invoke(initialState, config);

With a checkpointer attached, the exact same .invoke() call you've been using all along now also, transparently, saves a full snapshot of the graph's state after each step — which is what makes the audit features in 2.10 possible.

#2.10 State management and time travel

Once a run is checkpointed, three new operations become available, all scoped by the same thread_id:

getState(config) — returns the graph's current state (its latest checkpoint) for that thread: useful for "show me where this analysis run stands right now."

getStateHistory(config) — returns an async iterable walking backward through every checkpoint ever saved for that thread, oldest state visible last. This is your audit trail: each entry includes the state values at that point, which node produced it, and a checkpoint_id you can reference to jump back to that exact moment.

updateState(config, values, asNode) — this is "time travel." You give it a checkpoint_id (via config.configurable.checkpoint_id) pointing at a specific past checkpoint, plus new values to apply, plus asNode (the name of the node this correction should be attributed to, as if that node itself had produced this output). LangGraph creates a new checkpoint branching off from that point, with your corrected values applied. Critically, it does not touch or delete the original run's history — the original checkpoints remain, and you now additionally have a corrected fork.

Why this matters for a real product feature, not just a debugging tool: imagine a compliance reviewer disagrees with a HIGH risk score the model assigned to a clause. Instead of re-running the entire multi-clause analysis (expensive, slow, and would also silently re-score every other clause, potentially producing slightly different results due to LLM non-determinism), they can: look at the audit history, find the checkpoint right after that specific clause's score_clause step, call updateState with the corrected risk level at that checkpoint, and then resume the graph from there — everything downstream of that one clause (its routing decision, its contribution to aggregate_findings) re-runs using the corrected value, while every other clause's already-computed result is left completely untouched.

#2.11 Observability — why this phase is where tracing starts mattering

A graph this size (dynamic fan-out, conditional routing, multiple retriever layers per branch) is genuinely hard to debug by reading code alone — when a risk score looks wrong, you need to see which documents were retrieved, what the multi-query reformulations were, and what the model actually received in its final prompt. LangSmith tracing (enabled by three environment variables, no code changes required for basic tracing) captures every node execution, every LLM call, and every retriever call in a run as a browsable trace tree. This phase is the natural point to turn it on, because it's the first point where "just add a console.log and re-run" stops being a practical debugging strategy.

#3. Different Approaches

#3.1 Retriever upgrade — all at once vs. staged

Approach Pros Cons When to use
A. Build the full 4-layer stack immediately Less code churn (write it once) Much harder to isolate which layer is actually helping if something looks wrong; you never build the intuition for what each layer individually contributes If you're confident in the design and just want the end result
B. Build in stages — plain → MMR → Multi-Query → Contextual Compression, testing after each (roadmap's choice) You can directly compare retrieval quality/token cost before and after each addition; failures are easy to attribute to a specific layer More checkpoints to pause and manually verify along the way Recommended — matches your roadmap exactly, and this is a core phase worth understanding deeply rather than rushing

Recommendation: Approach B, exactly as specified. Section 6 below still gives you the complete final code for all four layers — "staged" here means validating each layer as you go, not withholding code.

#3.2 Clause processing — sequential loop vs. Send() fan-out

Approach Pros Cons When to use
A. Sequential loop — a single node that iterates clauses one at a time with a for loop, using a conditional self-edge to repeat Simpler graph structure; easier to reason about ordering Total latency scales linearly with clause count; doesn't exercise Send() at all, leaving that unit unpracticed; large contracts (40+ clauses) become slow Small contracts, or if strict ordering of clause processing genuinely matters (it doesn't here — clauses are independent)
B. Send()-based parallel fan-out (roadmap's choice) Latency roughly independent of clause count (bounded by API rate limits, not serial calls); directly exercises the map-reduce pattern central to LangGraph's design Slightly more moving parts (dispatch function, per-branch routing, join node); harder to reason about execution order if you're not used to thinking in terms of "supersteps" Recommended — clauses are fully independent of each other, exactly the situation Send() exists for

Recommendation: Approach B, as specified in your roadmap.

#3.3 Checkpointer backend

Approach Pros Cons When to use
A. MemorySaver (in-process, non-persistent) Zero setup, useful for quick local testing Checkpoints vanish when the process restarts — completely unusable for the audit-trail feature this phase requires, since a reviewer needs to inspect a run after the server has restarted Unit tests, quick local iteration only
B. PostgresSaver (persistent, in your existing database) Survives restarts; consistent with your "one database, not a zoo of specialized stores" architecture principle from Phase 1; audit history is genuinely durable Requires running the saver's setup step once to create its checkpoint tables Recommended — the audit/time-travel feature is a hard requirement of this phase, and only a persistent checkpointer can support it

Recommendation: Approach B. Use MemorySaver only in your unit tests, where a clean, ephemeral checkpointer is actually the better choice (no test pollution between runs).

#3.4 Audit/correction mechanism

Approach Pros Cons When to use
A. Full re-run — re-invoke the entire graph from scratch whenever a reviewer wants to correct one finding Simple to implement (no time-travel code needed) Wasteful (re-scores every clause, not just the corrected one); non-deterministic LLM output means other clauses' scores could subtly change too, which is actively undesirable for an audit feature meant to isolate one correction Acceptable only for very small contracts where cost/time genuinely don't matter
B. updateState-based partial revert (roadmap's choice) Only the corrected clause and anything genuinely downstream of it re-runs; every other clause's result is provably untouched; the original run's full history remains intact alongside the correction Requires understanding checkpoint IDs and the asNode parameter correctly Recommended — this is the actual point of Concept Learning 2.10, and it's a meaningfully better product feature, not just a more advanced one

#4. Detailed Implementation Plan

#Step 1 — Database schema

What: A risk_findings table (one row per finalized clause finding) and a clause_doc_group_map reference table (or an inline TypeScript constant — see Step 2) mapping clause types to relevant Phase 1 doc_group values. PostgresSaver's own checkpoint tables are created separately via its setup call, not hand-written SQL.

Why: risk_findings is the row Phase 5's report generator reads. Doc-group mapping is what keeps retrieval scoped and relevant — retrieving from your entire legal knowledge base for every clause regardless of type would both slow retrieval down and pollute context with irrelevant material (e.g. checking a payment-terms clause against labour law judgments).

Best practice: Store analysis_run_id (the graph's thread_id) on every risk_findings row — this is what lets the audit endpoints correlate a stored finding back to its exact checkpoint history.

#Step 2 — Clause-type → doc-group resolution

What: A lookup table mapping each clause_type (from Phase 3's CLAUSE_TYPES) to the doc_group values in legal_reference_chunks most relevant to it.

Why: This is the concrete mechanism that makes retrieval scoped rather than searching your whole knowledge base for every clause — directly answering the question Phase 2's classification stage set up ("which doc_groups are even relevant here").

Common mistake to avoid: Mapping too narrowly. A liability_indemnification clause might genuinely need both contract_law and, for an employment contract, labour_law context — err toward including a doc group if there's a plausible connection, since Contextual Compression (2.4) will filter out anything that turns out not to be relevant anyway.

#Step 3 — The retriever stack (four layers)

What: LegalKnowledgeBaseRetriever (base), MmrRetriever (wraps base), MultiQueryRetriever (wraps MMR), ContextualCompressionRetriever (wraps Multi-Query) — each a small class implementing the same BaseRetriever interface from Phase 2.5.

Why/How: Covered in Concept Learning 2.1–2.5.

Best practice: Log token counts before and after compression during your staged testing (Approach B in 3.1) — this is the concrete evidence for "is this layer actually helping," not just a theoretical improvement.

#Step 4 — Risk-scoring schema and chain

What: RiskFindingSchema (Zod) plus a prompt instructing the model to score risk only using the retrieved, compressed context, and a withStructuredOutput() chain.

Why: Same groundedness principle as Phase 2.5's answer chain — an ungrounded risk score is worse than no score at all in a legal-adjacent tool.

Best practice: Ask explicitly for the specific chunk(s) that justify the score (legalReferences) — this is both a groundedness forcing function (a model asked to cite its sources is measurably less likely to hallucinate a confident-sounding but ungrounded score) and directly useful output for Phase 5's report.

#Step 5 — Graph state schema

What: An Annotation.Root state covering contractId, analysisRunId, clauses (loaded from Phase 3's table, each augmented with resolved docGroups), currentClause (only present inside a Send-dispatched branch), and findings (concatenating reducer, same pattern as Phase 3's rawExtractedClauses).

Why: Same reasoning as Phase 3's state design — the concatenating reducer on findings is what lets every parallel score_clause/store_finding branch contribute to one shared final list without stepping on each other.

#Step 6 — resolve_doc_groups node

What: A single (non-parallel) node that loads the clause-type → doc-group mapping and attaches the resolved docGroups array to every clause in state.

Why: Doing this once, before fan-out, means each of the N parallel score_clause branches doesn't need to redundantly recompute the same mapping logic.

#Step 7 — Fan-out dispatch and the score_clause node

What: dispatchClauseScoring (the Send()-returning routing function) and score_clause (retrieves via the full stack, scores via the chain from Step 4, returns one finding).

Why/How: Covered in Concept Learning 2.6.

Common mistake to avoid: Forgetting that a Send-dispatched node's state is the global state with the Send payload merged on top — score_clause can and should still read shared fields like state.analysisRunId, not just state.currentClause.

#Step 8 — Conditional routing and the judgment_lookup stub

What: routeByRisk (HIGH → judgment_lookup, else → store_finding) and a stub judgment_lookup node that does a simple direct retrieval against your knowledge base's judgment-type documents — explicitly a placeholder, since Phase 4.5 replaces this exact node with a real agent that decides which tool to use rather than always doing the same fixed lookup.

Why: This is the concrete hook Phase 4.5 plugs into — building it as its own named node now, even in stub form, means swapping it for the agent later is a one-node replacement, not a graph restructuring.

#Step 9 — store_finding and aggregate_findings

What: store_finding inserts (or stages for insert) one finding; aggregate_findings is the join node every branch converges on, computing summary stats (counts per risk level) and marking the run complete.

Why: Covered in Concept Learning 2.8 — this is the reduce half of map-reduce.

#Step 10 — Checkpointer setup and graph compilation

What: Instantiate PostgresSaver, run its one-time .setup() call, and pass it to .compile({ checkpointer }).

Best practice: Run .setup() as part of your migration process (or a dedicated startup check), not on every server boot unconditionally — it's idempotent but there's no reason to call it more than once per environment.

#Step 11 — Audit endpoints

What: GET /api/analysis/:threadId/state, GET /api/analysis/:threadId/history, POST /api/analysis/:threadId/revert.

Why/How: Covered in Concept Learning 2.10.

Common mistake to avoid: Exposing raw checkpoint objects directly in the API response without shaping them — checkpoints contain internal LangGraph bookkeeping fields a frontend shouldn't need to understand; map them to a clean { checkpointId, node, timestamp, values } shape at the route layer.

#Step 12 — The orchestration endpoint and LangSmith setup

What: POST /api/contracts/:id/analysis/start — loads clauses, generates a fresh thread_id, invokes the compiled graph, returns the run ID immediately (or the full result, depending on whether you want this synchronous for now — Phase 7 adds real streaming/async progress).

Best practice: Return the analysisRunId (thread_id) in the response even if you return full results synchronously — the frontend and the audit endpoints both need it, and it costs nothing to include now.

#6.5 Base retriever

ts
// src/analysis/retrievers/base.ts
import { BaseRetriever, type BaseRetrieverInput } from "@langchain/core/retrievers";
import type { Document } from "@langchain/core/documents";
import { OpenAIEmbeddings } from "@langchain/openai";
import { Pool } from "pg";

const pool = new Pool({ connectionString: process.env.DATABASE_URL });
const embeddingsClient = new OpenAIEmbeddings({
  model: process.env.EMBEDDING_MODEL ?? "text-embedding-3-small",
});

export interface KnowledgeBaseRetrieverInput extends BaseRetrieverInput {
  docGroups: string[];
  fetchK: number;
}

export interface EmbeddedDocument extends Document {
  embedding: number[];
}

/**
 * The base layer of the retriever stack: plain cosine similarity search
 * against legal_reference_chunks, scoped to the given doc_groups - the
 * same shape as Phase 2.5's ContractRetriever, but querying the Phase 1
 * knowledge base instead of one contract's own chunks, and returning
 * embeddings alongside content so the MMR layer above can re-rank without
 * a second round of embedding calls.
 */
export class LegalKnowledgeBaseRetriever extends BaseRetriever {
  lc_namespace = ["legaldocs", "retrievers", "knowledge_base"];

  private docGroups: string[];
  private fetchK: number;

  constructor(fields: KnowledgeBaseRetrieverInput) {
    super(fields);
    this.docGroups = fields.docGroups;
    this.fetchK = fields.fetchK;
  }

  async fetchCandidatesWithEmbeddings(query: string): Promise<{
    queryEmbedding: number[];
    documents: EmbeddedDocument[];
  }> {
    const [queryEmbedding] = await embeddingsClient.embedDocuments([query]);

    const { rows } = await pool.query(
      `SELECT content, source_document, section_title, embedding,
              embedding <=> $1 AS distance
       FROM legal_reference_chunks
       WHERE doc_group = ANY($2::text[])
       ORDER BY embedding <=> $1
       LIMIT $3`,
      [`[${queryEmbedding.join(",")}]`, this.docGroups, this.fetchK]
    );

    const documents: EmbeddedDocument[] = rows.map((row) => ({
      pageContent: row.content,
      metadata: {
        sourceDocument: row.source_document,
        sectionTitle: row.section_title,
        distance: row.distance,
      },
      embedding: parsePgVector(row.embedding),
    }));

    return { queryEmbedding, documents };
  }

  async _getRelevantDocuments(query: string): Promise<Document[]> {
    const { documents } = await this.fetchCandidatesWithEmbeddings(query);
    return documents;
  }
}

/** pgvector returns embeddings as a "[0.1,0.2,...]" string literal via the
 *  driver unless explicitly cast - parse it back into a plain number array. */
function parsePgVector(raw: string): number[] {
  return raw
    .slice(1, -1)
    .split(",")
    .map(Number);
}

#6.6 MMR retriever

ts
// src/analysis/retrievers/mmr.ts
import { BaseRetriever, type BaseRetrieverInput } from "@langchain/core/retrievers";
import type { Document } from "@langchain/core/documents";
import { LegalKnowledgeBaseRetriever, type EmbeddedDocument } from "./base";

function cosineSimilarity(a: number[], b: number[]): number {
  let dot = 0, normA = 0, normB = 0;
  for (let i = 0; i < a.length; i++) {
    dot += a[i] * b[i];
    normA += a[i] * a[i];
    normB += b[i] * b[i];
  }
  return dot / (Math.sqrt(normA) * Math.sqrt(normB));
}

export interface MmrRetrieverInput extends BaseRetrieverInput {
  baseRetriever: LegalKnowledgeBaseRetriever;
  finalK: number;
  lambda: number;
}

/**
 * Wraps a base retriever with Maximal Marginal Relevance re-ranking: fetches
 * a larger candidate pool (base retriever's fetchK), then greedily selects
 * finalK documents balancing relevance to the query against dissimilarity
 * from documents already selected - so the final set isn't five near-
 * identical restatements of the same statute section.
 */
export class MmrRetriever extends BaseRetriever {
  lc_namespace = ["legaldocs", "retrievers", "mmr"];

  private baseRetriever: LegalKnowledgeBaseRetriever;
  private finalK: number;
  private lambda: number;

  constructor(fields: MmrRetrieverInput) {
    super(fields);
    this.baseRetriever = fields.baseRetriever;
    this.finalK = fields.finalK;
    this.lambda = fields.lambda;
  }

  async _getRelevantDocuments(query: string): Promise<Document[]> {
    const { queryEmbedding, documents } = await this.baseRetriever.fetchCandidatesWithEmbeddings(
      query
    );

    if (documents.length <= this.finalK) return documents;

    const selected: EmbeddedDocument[] = [];
    const remaining = [...documents];

    while (selected.length < this.finalK && remaining.length > 0) {
      let bestIndex = 0;
      let bestScore = -Infinity;

      for (let i = 0; i < remaining.length; i++) {
        const relevance = cosineSimilarity(remaining[i].embedding, queryEmbedding);
        const maxSimToSelected = selected.length === 0
          ? 0
          : Math.max(...selected.map((s) => cosineSimilarity(remaining[i].embedding, s.embedding)));

        const mmrScore = this.lambda * relevance - (1 - this.lambda) * maxSimToSelected;

        if (mmrScore > bestScore) {
          bestScore = mmrScore;
          bestIndex = i;
        }
      }

      selected.push(remaining[bestIndex]);
      remaining.splice(bestIndex, 1);
    }

    return selected;
  }
}

#6.7 Multi-Query retriever

ts
// src/analysis/retrievers/multiQuery.ts
import { BaseRetriever, type BaseRetrieverInput } from "@langchain/core/retrievers";
import type { Document } from "@langchain/core/documents";
import { ChatOpenAI } from "@langchain/openai";
import { ChatPromptTemplate } from "@langchain/core/prompts";
import { z } from "zod";
import { MmrRetriever } from "./mmr";

const model = new ChatOpenAI({
  model: process.env.RISK_SCORING_MODEL ?? "gpt-4o-mini",
  temperature: 0.3, // slight variety is desirable here - we WANT differently worded reformulations
});

const ReformulationSchema = z.object({
  reformulations: z
    .array(z.string())
    .describe("Alternative phrasings of the same underlying legal question, using different vocabulary"),
});

const reformulationPrompt = ChatPromptTemplate.fromMessages([
  [
    "system",
    "Generate {count} alternative phrasings of the following contract clause " +
      "concern, each using different legal vocabulary that a statute or judgment " +
      "might use to describe the same underlying issue. Do not answer the " +
      "question - only rephrase it.",
  ],
  ["human", "{query}"],
]);

const reformulationChain = reformulationPrompt.pipe(
  model.withStructuredOutput(ReformulationSchema, { name: "reformulate_query" })
);

export interface MultiQueryRetrieverInput extends BaseRetrieverInput {
  innerRetriever: MmrRetriever;
  reformulationCount: number;
}

/**
 * Wraps an inner retriever (here, the MMR layer) by generating several
 * differently-worded reformulations of the query, retrieving independently
 * for each, and returning the deduplicated union - so a vocabulary mismatch
 * between the clause's exact wording and the knowledge base's phrasing
 * doesn't cause an under-retrieval.
 */
export class MultiQueryRetriever extends BaseRetriever {
  lc_namespace = ["legaldocs", "retrievers", "multi_query"];

  private innerRetriever: MmrRetriever;
  private reformulationCount: number;

  constructor(fields: MultiQueryRetrieverInput) {
    super(fields);
    this.innerRetriever = fields.innerRetriever;
    this.reformulationCount = fields.reformulationCount;
  }

  async _getRelevantDocuments(query: string): Promise<Document[]> {
    const { reformulations } = await reformulationChain.invoke({
      query,
      count: this.reformulationCount,
    });

    const allQueries = [query, ...reformulations];

    const resultsPerQuery = await Promise.all(
      allQueries.map((q) => this.innerRetriever.invoke(q))
    );

    const seen = new Set<string>();
    const deduped: Document[] = [];

    for (const docs of resultsPerQuery) {
      for (const doc of docs) {
        const key = `${doc.metadata.sourceDocument}::${doc.pageContent.slice(0, 100)}`;
        if (!seen.has(key)) {
          seen.add(key);
          deduped.push(doc);
        }
      }
    }

    return deduped;
  }
}

#6.8 Contextual Compression retriever

ts
// src/analysis/retrievers/contextualCompression.ts
import { BaseRetriever, type BaseRetrieverInput } from "@langchain/core/retrievers";
import type { Document } from "@langchain/core/documents";
import { ChatOpenAI } from "@langchain/openai";
import { ChatPromptTemplate } from "@langchain/core/prompts";
import { z } from "zod";
import { MultiQueryRetriever } from "./multiQuery";

const model = new ChatOpenAI({
  model: process.env.RISK_SCORING_MODEL ?? "gpt-4o-mini",
  temperature: 0,
});

const ExtractionSchema = z.object({
  relevantExcerpt: z
    .string()
    .nullable()
    .describe("The verbatim portion of the document relevant to the query, or null if nothing is relevant"),
});

const extractionPrompt = ChatPromptTemplate.fromMessages([
  [
    "system",
    "Given a query and a document, extract ONLY the verbatim portion of the " +
      "document that is relevant to the query. If nothing in the document is " +
      "relevant, return null. Do not paraphrase or summarize - extract exact text.",
  ],
  ["human", "Query: {query}\n\nDocument:\n{document}"],
]);

const extractionChain = extractionPrompt.pipe(
  model.withStructuredOutput(ExtractionSchema, { name: "extract_relevant_excerpt" })
);

export interface ContextualCompressionRetrieverInput extends BaseRetrieverInput {
  innerRetriever: MultiQueryRetriever;
}

/**
 * The outermost layer of the retriever stack: compresses each surviving
 * document down to only its relevant excerpt (or drops it entirely if
 * nothing in it is actually on-point), so the final risk-scoring prompt
 * receives dense, on-topic legal text instead of full chunks padded with
 * irrelevant surrounding material.
 */
export class ContextualCompressionRetriever extends BaseRetriever {
  lc_namespace = ["legaldocs", "retrievers", "contextual_compression"];

  private innerRetriever: MultiQueryRetriever;

  constructor(fields: ContextualCompressionRetrieverInput) {
    super(fields);
    this.innerRetriever = fields.innerRetriever;
  }

  async _getRelevantDocuments(query: string): Promise<Document[]> {
    const docs = await this.innerRetriever.invoke(query);

    const compressed = await Promise.all(
      docs.map(async (doc) => {
        const { relevantExcerpt } = await extractionChain.invoke({
          query,
          document: doc.pageContent,
        });
        if (!relevantExcerpt) return null;
        return { ...doc, pageContent: relevantExcerpt };
      })
    );

    return compressed.filter((d): d is Document => d !== null);
  }
}

#6.9 Building the full stack for a clause

ts
// src/analysis/retrievers/index.ts
import { LegalKnowledgeBaseRetriever } from "./base";
import { MmrRetriever } from "./mmr";
import { MultiQueryRetriever } from "./multiQuery";
import { ContextualCompressionRetriever } from "./contextualCompression";

const FETCH_K = Number(process.env.RETRIEVER_FETCH_K ?? 20);
const FINAL_K = Number(process.env.RETRIEVER_FINAL_K ?? 5);
const MMR_LAMBDA = Number(process.env.RETRIEVER_MMR_LAMBDA ?? 0.6);
const REFORMULATION_COUNT = Number(process.env.MULTI_QUERY_REFORMULATIONS ?? 3);

/**
 * Composes the full 4-layer retriever stack for a given set of doc_groups.
 * Built fresh per clause since doc_groups differ per clause type - cheap to
 * construct, all the real cost is in the .invoke() calls themselves.
 */
export function buildRetrieverStack(docGroups: string[]) {
  const base = new LegalKnowledgeBaseRetriever({ docGroups, fetchK: FETCH_K });
  const mmr = new MmrRetriever({ baseRetriever: base, finalK: FINAL_K, lambda: MMR_LAMBDA });
  const multiQuery = new MultiQueryRetriever({
    innerRetriever: mmr,
    reformulationCount: REFORMULATION_COUNT,
  });
  return new ContextualCompressionRetriever({ innerRetriever: multiQuery });
}

import { ChatOpenAI } from "@langchain/openai";

import { buildRetrieverStack } from "./retrievers";

  model: process.env.RISK_SCORING_MODEL ?? "gpt-4o-mini",
  temperature: 0,
});

  [
    "system",
    "You are a legal risk analyst. Assess the risk of the following contract " +
      "clause using ONLY the provided legal context excerpts - do not rely on " +
      "general knowledge beyond what's given. If the context does not clearly " +
      "support a HIGH risk assessment, do not assign one. Every legal reference " +
      "you cite must correspond to an excerpt actually provided below.\n\n" +
      "Legal context:\n{context}",
  ],
  ["human", "Clause type: {clauseType}\nClause text:\n{clauseText}"],
]);

  model.withStructuredOutput(RiskFindingSchema, { name: "score_clause_risk" })
);

function formatContext(docs: Document[]): string {
  if (docs.length === 0) return "(No relevant legal context was found for this clause.)";
  return docs
    .map((d, i) => `[Reference ${i + 1} - ${d.metadata.sourceDocument}]\n${d.pageContent}`)
    .join("\n\n");
}

export async function scoreClauseRisk(
  clauseType: string,
  clauseText: string,
  docGroups: string[]
) {
  const retriever = buildRetrieverStack(docGroups);
  const docs = await retriever.invoke(`${clauseType}: ${clauseText}`);

  const finding = await scoringChain.invoke({
    clauseType,
    clauseText,
    context: formatContext(docs),
  });

  return { finding, retrievedDocs: docs };
}

    counts

  return {};

import { PostgresSaver } from "@langchain/langgraph-checkpoint-postgres";
import { AnalysisState } from "./state";
import { resolveDocGroupsNode } from "./nodes/resolveDocGroups";
import { dispatchClauseScoring } from "./nodes/dispatch";
import { scoreClauseNode } from "./nodes/scoreClause";
import { judgmentLookupStubNode } from "./nodes/judgmentLookupStub";
import { storeFindingNode } from "./nodes/storeFinding";
import { aggregateFindingsNode } from "./nodes/aggregateFindings";
import { routeByRisk } from "./routing";

/**
 * Call this once at application startup - it creates PostgresSaver's own
 * checkpoint tables if they don't already exist. Idempotent, but there's no
 * reason to call it more than once per environment.
 */
export async function setupCheckpointer(): Promise<void> {
  await checkpointer.setup();
}

const graph = new StateGraph(AnalysisState)
  .addNode("resolve_doc_groups", resolveDocGroupsNode)
  .addNode("score_clause", scoreClauseNode)
  .addNode("judgment_lookup", judgmentLookupStubNode)
  .addNode("store_finding", storeFindingNode)
  .addNode("aggregate_findings", aggregateFindingsNode)
  .addEdge(START, "resolve_doc_groups")
  // Send()-based dynamic fan-out: one score_clause execution per clause.
  .addConditionalEdges("resolve_doc_groups", dispatchClauseScoring)
  // Per-branch conditional routing by risk level.
  .addConditionalEdges("score_clause", routeByRisk, {
    judgment_lookup: "judgment_lookup",
    store_finding: "store_finding",
  })
  .addEdge("judgment_lookup", "store_finding")
  // Every branch converges here - the reduce/join step.
  .addEdge("store_finding", "aggregate_findings")
  .addEdge("aggregate_findings", END);

export const analysisGraph = graph.compile({ checkpointer });

  analysisRunId: string,
  finding: RiskFindingRecord
): Promise<void> {
  await pool.query(
    `INSERT INTO risk_findings
       (contract_id, clause_id, analysis_run_id, risk_level, risk_score,
        reasoning, fix_suggestion, legal_references)
     VALUES ($1, $2, $3, $4, $5, $6, $7, $8)`,
    [
      contractId,
      finding.clauseId,
      analysisRunId,
      finding.riskLevel,
      finding.riskScore,
      finding.reasoning,
      finding.fixSuggestion,
      JSON.stringify(finding.legalReferences),
    ]
  );
}

export async function getFindingsForRun(analysisRunId: string) {
  const { rows } = await pool.query(
    `SELECT * FROM risk_findings WHERE analysis_run_id = $1 ORDER BY created_at ASC`,
    [analysisRunId]
  );
  return rows;
}

#6.16 Orchestration and audit routes

ts
// src/routes/analysis.ts
import { Router } from "express";
import { randomUUID } from "crypto";
import { Pool } from "pg";
import { analysisGraph } from "../analysis/graph";
import { getFindingsForRun } from "../analysis/storage";

const pool = new Pool({ connectionString: process.env.DATABASE_URL });
export const analysisRouter = Router();

analysisRouter.post("/contracts/:id/analysis/start", async (req, res) => {
  const { id } = req.params;

  const { rows } = await pool.query(
    `SELECT id, clause_type, title, full_text FROM clauses WHERE contract_id = $1`,
    [id]
  );
  if (rows.length === 0) {
    return res.status(404).json({ error: "No extracted clauses found for this contract." });
  }

  const analysisRunId = randomUUID();
  const config = { configurable: { thread_id: analysisRunId } };

  try {
    await analysisGraph.invoke(
      {
        contractId: id,
        analysisRunId,
        clauses: rows.map((r) => ({
          id: r.id,
          clauseType: r.clause_type,
          title: r.title,
          fullText: r.full_text,
          docGroups: [],
        })),
      },
      config
    );

    const findings = await getFindingsForRun(analysisRunId);
    return res.status(201).json({ analysisRunId, findings });
  } catch (err) {
    console.error("Risk analysis failed:", err);
    return res.status(500).json({ error: "Analysis failed.", analysisRunId });
  }
});

analysisRouter.get("/analysis/:threadId/state", async (req, res) => {
  const config = { configurable: { thread_id: req.params.threadId } };
  const state = await analysisGraph.getState(config);
  return res.status(200).json({ values: state.values, next: state.next });
});

analysisRouter.get("/analysis/:threadId/history", async (req, res) => {
  const config = { configurable: { thread_id: req.params.threadId } };
  const history = [];

  for await (const checkpoint of analysisGraph.getStateHistory(config)) {
    history.push({
      checkpointId: checkpoint.config.configurable?.checkpoint_id,
      node: checkpoint.metadata?.source,
      values: checkpoint.values,
    });
  }

  return res.status(200).json({ history });
});

analysisRouter.post("/analysis/:threadId/revert", async (req, res) => {
  const { threadId } = req.params;
  const { checkpointId, correctedFinding } = req.body;

  const config = {
    configurable: { thread_id: threadId, checkpoint_id: checkpointId },
  };

  // Creates a new checkpoint branching off the given point, as if the
  // "store_finding" node itself had produced this corrected value - the
  // original run's history remains fully intact alongside this fork.
  await analysisGraph.updateState(config, { currentFinding: correctedFinding }, "store_finding");

  // Resume execution from the corrected checkpoint onward.
  const result = await analysisGraph.invoke(null, config);

  return res.status(200).json({ resumed: true, result });
});

#Request / execution flow

text
POST /api/contracts/:id/analysis/start
  │
  ▼
Load clauses for contract (404 if none extracted yet — run Phase 3 first)
  │
  ▼
analysisGraph.invoke({ contractId, analysisRunId, clauses }, { thread_id })
  │
  ├─ resolve_doc_groups                     — attach doc_groups per clause
  │
  ├─ Send() dispatch → score_clause (× N)   — parallel, one per clause
  │      each: retriever stack (base→MMR→MultiQuery→Compression) → LLM score
  │
  ├─ per branch: routeByRisk
  │      HIGH   → judgment_lookup (stub) → store_finding
  │      other  → store_finding directly
  │
  ├─ ALL branches converge → aggregate_findings   (runs once)
  │
  ▼
HTTP 201 { analysisRunId, findings }

Later, independently:
GET  /api/analysis/:threadId/state     → current checkpoint
GET  /api/analysis/:threadId/history   → every checkpoint, in order
POST /api/analysis/:threadId/revert    → fork + resume from a past checkpoint

#Why this design over alternatives

  • Send() fan-out over a sequential loop: clauses are fully independent; there's no correctness reason to score them one at a time, and doing so would make large-contract analysis latency scale linearly with clause count for no benefit.
  • Decorator-composed retrievers, each independently testable: you can disable/swap any single layer (e.g. test with MMR but without Multi-Query) without touching the others, which is exactly what the staged-build recommendation in section 3.1 depends on.
  • PostgresSaver over MemorySaver in production: the audit/time-travel feature is a hard product requirement here, not an optional nicety, and only a persistent checkpointer supports inspecting a run after the process has restarted.
  • A named judgment_lookup stub node, not inline logic inside score_clause: Phase 4.5 needs a clean seam to replace fixed lookup logic with real agent reasoning — giving it its own node now means that swap doesn't require touching the graph's edges at all.

#8. Best Practices

Industry best practices

  • Ground every risk finding strictly in retrieved context, and require the model to cite the specific excerpts it used (legalReferences) — this is both a quality and an auditability requirement for a legal-adjacent tool.
  • Treat the checkpointer the same way you treat any other shared client — instantiate once, reuse across requests.
  • Keep temperature: 0 for the risk-scoring, compression-extraction, and routing-relevant chains; only the Multi-Query reformulation step benefits from a touch of variety (0.3), and only there.

Scalability considerations

  • Send()-based fan-out means analysis latency for a contract scales with the slowest individual clause's retrieval+scoring time, not the sum of all clauses — a meaningful win once you're processing 40+ clause contracts.
  • Watch your OpenAI rate limits once contract volume grows: many parallel score_clause branches, each running 4 retriever layers plus a scoring call, can burst a large number of concurrent API calls. A concurrency limiter (e.g. p-limit wrapping the retriever stack's LLM calls) is worth adding before this goes to real production load, even though it's not strictly necessary for correctness at moderate volume.

Security considerations

  • Never log full clause text or full legal excerpts to application logs — log clause IDs, risk levels, and counts only, consistent with every prior phase's confidentiality discipline.
  • thread_ids are effectively access tokens to a specific analysis run's full history — validate that the requesting user actually owns the underlying contract_id before serving /analysis/:threadId/* endpoints once auth exists (same open item noted in Phase 2.5).

Performance optimizations

  • Contextual Compression trims token usage right before the most expensive part of the pipeline (the final risk-scoring call) — log before/after token counts during your staged testing to confirm it's earning its keep on your real corpus, not just in theory.
  • Building the retriever stack fresh per clause is cheap (it's just object construction); the real cost is entirely in the .invoke() calls, so there's no need to cache or pool retriever instances themselves.

Maintainability tips

  • Keep routeByRisk and dispatchClauseScoring as small, standalone, named functions — they're the two places graph structure depends on runtime data, and they deserve to be easy to find and unit test in isolation from the nodes themselves.
  • Every retriever layer implements the exact same interface — when Phase 6.5 needs a variant retrieval strategy for a different specialist agent, it's a new decorator, not a rewrite.

Testing considerations

  • Unit test MmrRetriever with a small fixture of known embeddings where you can hand-calculate the expected selection order — assert it actually diversifies rather than just re-sorting by similarity.
  • Unit test routeByRisk and dispatchClauseScoring directly against constructed state objects, with no real graph or LLM involved.
  • Integration test the full graph against a small fixture contract with a mix of clause types, using MemorySaver (not PostgresSaver) for test isolation — assert findings.length === clauses.length and that at least one HIGH-risk clause (crafted into the fixture) routed through judgment_lookup.
  • Explicitly test updateState + resume against a completed test run — assert the corrected clause's finding changes while every other finding's risk_score and reasoning remain byte-for-byte identical to the pre-correction run.

#9. Summary

What we learned:

  • How MMR, Multi-Query, and Contextual Compression retrievers each solve a distinct weakness of plain similarity search, and how they compose cleanly as decorators around the same basic retriever interface from Phase 2.5.
  • Send() as the graph-native way to dynamically fan a node out over a runtime-determined list — the direct successor to Phase 3's Promise.all, now expressed as a first-class, traceable, conditionally-routable part of the graph itself.
  • Conditional edges (addConditionalEdges) as LangGraph's equivalent of Phase 2's RunnableBranch, and how multiple parallel branches converging on a shared downstream node is what makes the map-reduce join actually work, backed by a concatenating reducer.
  • What a checkpointer is actually for: not just fault tolerance, but making a graph run durably inspectable — thread_id-scoped state that survives past the original request/response cycle.
  • getState, getStateHistory, and updateState as a genuine product feature (auditable, surgical correction of one finding) rather than a debugging convenience — and why forking from a checkpoint, rather than re-running the whole graph, is the only way to correct one clause without risking silent changes to every other clause's result.

What has been completed:

  • The full 4-layer retriever stack (base, MMR, Multi-Query, Contextual Compression).
  • RiskFindingSchema and a grounded, citation-requiring risk-scoring chain.
  • A compiled, checkpointed LangGraph graph implementing Send()-based parallel clause scoring, conditional HIGH-risk routing to a judgment_lookup stub, and a map-reduce join into aggregate_findings.
  • risk_findings table and storage layer.
  • Orchestration (POST /analysis/start) and full audit endpoints (state, history, revert) built on getState/getStateHistory/updateState.

What is left:

  • Running the hands-on tasks against real contracts of varying length and clause diversity, validating both retrieval quality (via the staged-build comparisons) and the time-travel correction flow end to end (tasks 15-17 — yours to execute).
  • Everything from Phase 4.5 onward, which builds directly on top of this phase's graph and output.

How this connects to the next phase: Phase 4.5 (Legal Research Agent) replaces exactly one thing in this phase's graph: the body of the judgment_lookup node. Right now, it's a stub that always does the same fixed similarity search. Phase 4.5 rebuilds it as a real agent — first as a hand-rolled LangChain AgentExecutor with a ReAct loop, then as a LangGraph createReactAgent() + ToolNode — that decides for itself, per HIGH-risk clause, whether to search Indian Kanoon, search general case precedent, or flag the clause for human review. Because this phase built judgment_lookup as its own clearly-scoped node rather than inline logic, that upgrade slots into the exact same graph position, with every other node, edge, and the checkpointer/audit infrastructure completely unchanged.
